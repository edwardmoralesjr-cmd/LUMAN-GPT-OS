# Relational Perspective - Research Support

## Status

Recovered evidence-companion snapshot. This file preserves the research map developed to support the relational expansion while maintaining the distinction between empirical findings, interpretive models, historical analogy, and original synthesis.

## Evidence-Use Rule

No research area below proves the complete Immediate Field framework. The evidence clarifies mechanisms, boundaries, and responsible claims. Practices remain open to revision through stronger evidence and lived consequence.

## Embodied Regulation and the Sacred Pause

Research on slow breathing and breathwork supports short-term physiological regulation and stress reduction. Systematic reviews have reported relationships with heart-rate variability, autonomic balance, and mental-health outcomes. Context and individual differences matter.

The practical application is modest: inserting a repeatable pause before interpretation or response may increase the likelihood of connection-supporting behavior during activated family moments.

## Narrative Identity and Co-Creation

Narrative-identity research associated with Dan McAdams supports the view that people construct evolving life stories that provide continuity, purpose, and identity. These stories are shaped within social and relational contexts.

Berger and Luckmann's social-construction framework supports the claim that shared meanings, roles, and institutional realities are maintained through repeated social recognition.

## Shared Reading and Child Development

Research on shared reading and dialogic reading supports benefits involving language development, emotional vocabulary, joint attention, responsive interaction, and theory of mind. The Immediate Field extends this evidence into a practical hypothesis: a regulated adult's presence changes the relational quality through which story is transmitted.

## Self-Compassion and Non-Contingent Worth

Kristin Neff's self-compassion framework includes self-kindness, common humanity, and mindful awareness rather than over-identification. Research commonly associates self-compassion with lower distress and healthier coping.

The Core Directive aligns with this work while adding a sovereignty dimension:

> I am already worthy, and from that worthiness I will build.

This is not a scientific conclusion about metaphysical worth. It is a practical and philosophical ground for discipline without shame-based identity.

## Self-Determination Theory

Ryan and Deci's self-determination theory identifies autonomy, competence, and relatedness as central psychological needs. This supports the framework's insistence that sovereignty should strengthen genuine relatedness rather than become isolation, enmeshment, or control.

## Conflict, Repair, and Attachment

Couples and attachment research supports the importance of responsiveness, de-escalation, bids for connection, responsibility, and repair after rupture. The framework's Directional Engine and Fracture Mapping are practical synthesis tools, not replacements for validated couples therapy or professional care.

## Historical and Institutional Analogy

The Hospitaller example is used as a historical analogy for adapting outer form while preserving purpose. Broader civilizational-cycle theories from Polybius, Ibn Khaldun, Vico, Toynbee, Glubb, and others are interpretive traditions rather than settled predictive laws.

## Habit, Feedback, and Iteration

Implementation intentions, deliberate practice, habit formation, feedback loops, and small repeatable actions support the Six-Command Daily Loop and weekly review structure. The framework should favor observable behavior and correction over identity-level shame.

## Hawthorne Effect Boundary

Research participation and awareness can alter behavior in some settings, but the Hawthorne effect is heterogeneous and its mechanisms and magnitude are not fully settled. The framework therefore uses awareness as a possible intervention point, not a guaranteed cause of lasting change.
