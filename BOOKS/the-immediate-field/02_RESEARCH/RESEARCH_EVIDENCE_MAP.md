# The Immediate Field - Research and Evidence Map

## Purpose

This file protects the book from presenting all claims at the same level of certainty.

## Evidence Classes

### A. Strong or Established Research Base

Use careful but confident language where supported:

- Slow breathing and physiological arousal
- Self-compassion and well-being
- Self-determination theory: autonomy, competence, and relatedness
- Narrative identity as a psychological research program
- Attachment, co-regulation, and relational repair
- Implementation intentions, feedback loops, and small repeatable behavior change

### B. Active or Contested Research Programs

Use qualified language and name limitations:

- Predictive processing and free-energy formulations
- Polyvagal theory as a broad explanatory framework
- Hawthorne effects and mechanisms of observation-induced behavior change
- Broad claims about nervous-system interpretation of distant mediated threat

### C. Historical Interpretation and Analogy

Use reputable historical sources and avoid presenting analogy as proof:

- Knights Templar narrative collapse
- Knights Hospitaller institutional adaptability
- Civilizational cycles in Polybius, Ibn Khaldun, Vico, Toynbee, Glubb, and others
- Religion as trust architecture and costly signaling

### D. Comparative Mythology and Spiritual Traditions

Do not flatten traditions into identical doctrines. Treat them as distinct cultural models that may illuminate recurring human observations:

- Foreign Installation
- Wetiko
- Nafs
- Logismoi
- Mara
- Archons
- Maya

### E. Edward's Original Synthesis

Label these as the book's philosophical and practical framework:

- The Immediate Field
- The Five Fields of Agency
- The three distances of agency
- The Directional Engine
- The Emotional Firewall
- The Sacred Pause
- The Hospitaller Protocol
- Fracture Mapping
- The Core Directive
- The framework must remain able to lose

## Evidence Workflow Per Chapter

For every completed chapter:

1. List factual and empirical claims.
2. Identify which claims need citation.
3. Record the strongest primary or scholarly sources.
4. Mark contested claims explicitly.
5. Separate metaphor from mechanism.
6. Confirm that practice recommendations remain proportionate to the evidence.
7. Add medical, mental-health, legal, or safety boundaries where needed.
